## Brief

Pick any real product you use — a website, a mobile app, an internal tool, a CLI. Spend 30 minutes auditing it as a QA engineer, not as a user. Write the field report another teammate would expect on Monday.

The point is **investigator mindset**, not exhaustive coverage. You're practicing the muscles every Cluster-1 topic builds: oracle-based reasoning, functional-vs-experiential thinking, and risk-driven prioritization. The artifact is short on purpose — a field report, not a test plan.

## Suggested approach

1. **Pick a target.** Anything with a published claim ("the fastest way to…", "always saves your work…") is fair game — claims give you oracles to test against.
2. **Set a timer for 30 minutes.** When it rings, stop investigating and start writing.
3. **Explore actively.** Don't follow the happy path. Try inputs the product probably doesn't expect; resize the window; toggle dark mode; lose your connection mid-action. Note what surprised you.
4. **Name your oracles as you go.** *"This violates consistency-with-claims — the landing page says 'instant', the action took 4s."* Naming forces clarity.
5. **Catalog findings under two headings:** Functional (does it work) and Experiential (is it pleasant/trustworthy). A single observation can sit under both.
6. **Rank by likelihood × impact.** Likelihood = how many users hit it. Impact = severity if they do. One sentence of rationale per rank.
7. **Write the executive summary last.** One paragraph. Pretend the reader is a PM who has 45 seconds before their next meeting.

## What "done" looks like

A single markdown document with:

- **Executive summary** — one paragraph, non-engineer-readable.
- **Method** — what product, what you tried, which oracles you reached for.
- **Findings** — each with repro steps, expected, actual, proposed next step, and a rank (likelihood × impact + rationale).
- **What I didn't investigate** — one line on what you deprioritized and why.

No screenshots required. No bug-tracker tickets. The artifact is the writeup.

## Why this matters

Every later cluster — test design, automation, non-functional, AI/LLM QA — is downstream of the skills in Cluster 1: noticing, naming, and prioritizing. A QA engineer who can produce a clean field report on a product they've never seen before is immediately credible on any team, regardless of what stack you end up using.

This is also the cheapest project in the curriculum to repeat. Once you've done one, doing a second on a different product takes half the time and teaches twice as much.
