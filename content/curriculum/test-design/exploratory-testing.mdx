## Core Idea

Exploratory testing (ET) is **simultaneous learning, test design, and test execution, governed by a charter and a time-box**. It is not ad-hoc testing — the difference is governance. Ad-hoc testing has no charter, no time-box, no notes, no debrief; ET has all four.

In scripted testing, learning, design, execution, and interpretation are *sequential*. In ET they are *coupled*: what the tester discovers in minute one changes the test designed in minute two, which changes the interpretation in minute three. This coupling — not the absence of scripts — is what produces unique-to-ET bug finds that no pre-written test would have caught.

ET is the discipline that addresses **unknown-unknowns**: bugs no spec anticipated, paths no risk register named. Scripted tests find designed-for bugs; exploratory testing finds the surprises that designed-for tests cannot reach. The two are complements, not substitutes. See [[qa-mindset]] for the testing-vs-checking distinction that frames why ET requires a human.

> Exploratory testing is systematic investigation governed by a charter — not freestyle clicking. The charter is the work.

## Worked Example

### Charter quality decides session value

Two testers, same feature (user registration), 60 minutes each.

**Tester A receives:** "Test the registration page."

**Tester B receives the charter:** *"Explore the registration page's interaction with already-existing accounts — same email, same phone number, same OAuth-linked Google identity — using both the Sign-Up and Sign-Up-with-Google paths, to discover surprising error messages or account-linking behaviour. 60 minutes."*

After the session, Tester A produces: five bug reports about form validation and missing field labels.

Tester B produces: three bugs Tester A also found, plus a critical finding — signing up with a Google account whose email already exists in the system silently creates a duplicate account instead of prompting to link. Neither the spec nor any scripted regression test covered this path because nobody knew to look.

**What the charter gave Tester B:**
- **Mission**: discover account-linking surprises (not "verify the form works")
- **Target**: already-existing accounts via two sign-up paths
- **Oracle hint**: error messages and account-linking behaviour (connects to [[test-oracles-and-prioritization]] — which oracle type are we testing against?)
- **Time-box**: 60 minutes of focused exploration

The charter is the work. Without it, ET is ad-hoc testing with a better name.

### TBS timing as a diagnostic

Tester B's session log shows: T=55%, B=35%, S=10%.

The high B percentage is a signal: bugs were found faster than expected and took time to investigate. The test lead's response at debrief: reduce the next session's time-box, assign a bug-investigation session to a second tester, and draft a charter focused specifically on the account-state edge cases the first session surfaced. This is SBTM working correctly — the debrief converts the session from "the tester's private experience" into "the team's next decision."

## Common Pitfalls

- **Calling ad-hoc testing "exploratory."** Ad-hoc has no charter, no time-box, no notes, no debrief. ET has all four. Without the floor, "exploratory doesn't work for us" becomes team folklore — because what they actually tried was wandering. Fix: adopt all four elements as the minimum contract before calling a session exploratory.
- **Vague charters.** "Explore the cart" is not a charter; it is a location. A charter names mission, target, oracle hint, and time-box. Fix: refuse to start a session on a charter that lacks any of the four. Why it happens: writing a good charter takes 5–10 minutes; most teams skip the discipline under time pressure.
- **Optimising solely for bug count.** Bug count steers ET toward shallow, repeated finds — the tester learns to file many small bugs rather than pursue deep surprises. Fix: track *questions raised* (things the team had not thought to ask) and *unique finds* (bugs no scripted test would have caught) alongside raw bug count. Why it happens: bug count is the only ET metric most dashboards surface.
- **Treating session notes as test cases.** ET notes are *evidence* of what happened — time-stamped, path-aware, question-recording. They are not re-runnable scripts. Converting them to scripted tests destroys the continuity-of-thought that produced the finds. Fix: keep notes as evidence artefacts; create separate scripted tests for the bugs confirmed. Why it happens: teams want to "operationalise" ET output and reach for the nearest tool (a test case template).
- **Using tours as a playbook.** Whittaker's product tours (feature tour, money tour, back-alley tour, etc.) are a charter-idea menu for when the tester is stuck — not a procedure to execute in order. Routine touring produces fatigue and shallow coverage. Fix: treat tours as a brainstorming device; stop when you have a real charter. Why it happens: tours are concrete and enumerable, which makes them feel like a safe process.
- **Solo-only ET.** Two testers per session — one driving, one noting, rotating every 20 minutes — reliably produces more notes, more questions raised, and more "I never would have noticed that" bugs. Fix: run at least one paired session per sprint. Why it happens: ET is perceived as a solo discipline because the charter belongs to one person.
- **Skipping the debrief.** An un-debriefed session is value locked in one tester's head. Even 5 minutes of structured debrief (what did you do, what surprised you, what charter next) converts it into a team record. Fix: treat the debrief as mandatory, not optional. Why it happens: testers move directly to the next session; the debrief feels like overhead when the backlog is full.

## Practice Task

<PracticeTask id="et-task-1" rubric="et-rubric-v1">
  Pick a real product — this site, any public web app, or a local application. Complete one full SBTM cycle and submit the following five artefacts.

  **1. The charter.** One paragraph. Must include: mission (what you are exploring *for*, not just *where*), target (the specific feature, path, or interaction), oracle hint (what kind of wrong would you recognise? — connect to [[test-oracles-and-prioritization]]), and time-box (60–90 minutes). The charter fails if the mission is "explore X" without naming what you are looking for.

  **2. Session notes.** Time-stamped (you may tidy them after, but the time signal must be honest). Record: the path taken, what surprised you, what you investigated, questions you raised that the spec does not answer, bugs you filed. At least two lines must be questions (not findings) — question-density is the best proxy for ET depth.

  **3. TBS timing breakdown.** Three percentages summing to 100: T (test design and execution), B (bug investigation), S (setup and overhead). Add one sentence interpreting the split honestly — what does it tell you about where this session spent its energy?

  **4. Bug reports.** Standard form for each bug found. (Reference [[test-oracles-and-prioritization]] — what oracle type applies to each bug?)

  **5. Debrief artefact.** Write the debrief as if you were the test lead recording another tester's session. Answer: what did the tester do? What surprised them? What questions remain open? What charter would you assign next — and is it derived from a session surprise or from the spec?

  Rubric (revealed after submission):
  - Did the charter pass all four axes (mission, target, oracle hint, time-box)? Missing any one is a fail.
  - Were at least 2 session-note lines questions rather than findings? If not, you may have done careful execution rather than exploration.
  - Does the TBS split feel honest — or reverse-engineered to look balanced? The lesson is honesty, not balance.
  - Did at least one bug come from a path the spec would not have suggested?
  - Does your "next charter" come from a session surprise — or from the spec? A surprise-derived charter is the continuity-of-thought signal that ET is working.
</PracticeTask>
